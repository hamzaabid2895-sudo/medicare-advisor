Understanding Medicare

Medicare Parts A, B, C, and D — Simply Explained

Medicare can feel like alphabet soup. Here's what each part actually means for your health and your wallet:

A

Hospital Coverage

Covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facilities, hospice, and some home health care. Most people get Part A at no cost.

B

Medical Coverage

Covers doctor visits, outpatient services, lab tests, and preventive care. Part B has a monthly premium (a regular payment you make).

C

Medicare Advantage

A private plan that bundles Parts A and B — often with drug coverage and extra benefits like dental, vision, and hearing.

D

Drug Coverage

Covers prescription medications. Private insurance companies offer Part D plans and vary in cost and coverage.

Together, these parts make up your Medicare coverage. The big question is: which combination is right for you? That's exactly what we help you figure out at no cost.

Medicare Advantage Plans: More Coverage, Often for Less

Your "All-in-One" Medicare option with extra benefits you won't find in Original Medicare.

Part C Explained

What Is a Medicare Advantage Plan?

Medicare Advantage, also called Part C, is a popular choice over Original Medicare. These plans bundle your hospital, doctor, and drug coverage into one simple plan, managed by private insurance companies.

Think of it as an "all-in-one" Medicare plan. Instead of managing separate cards and bills, you get one plan that typically includes:

🏥

Part A Coverage

Everything covered by Original Medicare Part A (Hospital care).

👨‍⚕️

Part B Coverage

Everything covered by Original Medicare Part B (Doctor visits).

💊

Part D (Drugs)

Prescription drug coverage is included in most plans.

The Perks

Extra Benefits That Can Make a Real Difference

This is where Medicare Advantage plans really shine. Most plans include benefits that Original Medicare simply does not cover:

🦷

Dental Coverage

Routine care like cleanings and X-rays, plus coverage for fillings, and sometimes even dentures.

👓

Vision Coverage

Annual eye exams and allowances for eyeglasses or contact lenses to keep your sight sharp.

👂

Hearing Coverage

Hearing tests and significant allowances for hearing aids, which can cost thousands out-of-pocket.

🏃‍♂️

Fitness & More

Memberships like SilverSneakers, transportation to doctors, and over-the-counter (OTC) health supply allowances.

Things to Know Before You Enroll

Advantage plans are a great fit for many, but they have specific rules to be aware of:

Network Restrictions: Most plans require you to use their network of doctors and hospitals. We'll check if your current doctors are included!

Out-of-Pocket Maximum: Unlike Original Medicare, these plans have a cap on how much you pay each year. Once you hit the limit, you pay 0% for the rest of the year.

Referrals: Depending on the plan type (like an HMO), you might need a referral from your primary doctor to see a specialist.

Medigap: Stop Worrying About the Bills Medicare Doesn't Cover

Fill the 20% gap left by Original Medicare and gain total freedom of choice.

What Are the "Gaps" in Original Medicare?

Original Medicare (Parts A and B) covers a lot, but it typically charges you for 20% of your medical costs, with no cap on how much that can add up to.

Example: If you have a $50,000 surgery and Medicare pays 80%, you're still on the hook for $10,000. A Medigap plan covers that 20% for you.

Depending on the plan you choose, Medigap can cover your deductibles, coinsurance, and even emergency care outside the U.S.

The Freedom to See Any Doctor You Want

One of the biggest advantages of a Medigap plan is flexibility. Unlike Medicare Advantage, there are no networks to worry about.

  • No referrals required to see a specialist.
  • See any doctor in the U.S. who accepts Medicare (which is most of them).
  • Perfect for travelers or "snowbirds" who live in different states.
Plan Types

Thinking About Changing Your Medicare Plan? You Have More Options Than You Think.

Every year, thousands of seniors discover they're paying too much or missing coverage. We're here to help.

Why Seniors Switch Medicare Plans

There are many good reasons to consider switching your Medicare plan, including:

  • Rising Premiums: Your monthly cost went up and you want a better value.
  • Doctor Access: Your preferred doctor is no longer in your plan's network.
  • Better Benefits: A new plan in your area offers improved dental, vision, or hearing.
  • Health Changes: Your needs have changed and your current plan no longer fits.
  • Drug Costs: You found a plan with lower out-of-pocket costs for your prescriptions.

Whatever your reason, you are not locked in forever. Medicare gives you specific windows to review and change your coverage.

Timing is Everything

When Can You Change Your Medicare Plan?

Oct 15 – Dec 7

Annual Enrollment (AEP)

The main window where anyone can switch from Original Medicare to Advantage, or change between Advantage/Part D plans.

Jan 1 – Mar 31

MA Open Enrollment

If you're already on an Advantage plan, you can switch to a different one or return to Original Medicare.

Year-Round

Special Enrollment (SEP)

Triggered by major life events like moving, losing employer coverage, or qualifying for state assistance.

Switching from Medicare Advantage Back to a Medigap Plan

This is one of the most common situations we help seniors navigate — and one of the trickiest. If you want to go back to Original Medicare and add a Medigap plan, you may face **medical underwriting** in most states.

The Exception: If you are within your Medigap Open Enrollment Period (the 6 months after starting Part B), you have "guaranteed issue rights"—meaning no company can deny you coverage or charge you more based on your health history.

Medicare Special Enrollment Period: What It Is and How It Works

Missed the standard window? A major life change might give you a second chance to enroll without penalties.

Most people sign up for Medicare during a set enrollment window. But life doesn't always follow a schedule. If you missed a window or if something in your life has recently changed, you may qualify for a Special Enrollment Period (SEP).

Common Events That Qualify for an SEP

💼

Losing Employer Coverage

Retiring or leaving a job? You have an 8-month window to enroll in Part B without a late penalty.

🏠

Moving Address

Moving to a new ZIP code or county where your current plan isn't available? You typically have 60 days to switch.

🛡️

Losing Medicaid

If you lose eligibility for state medical assistance, you gain a window to adjust your Medicare coverage.

🏥

Plan Contract Issues

If your plan leaves your area or loses its Medicare contract, you get an SEP to select a new one.

Avoid Costly Late Enrollment Penalties

If you miss your SEP or don't have a qualifying event, you could face permanent lifetime penalties:

Part B Penalty

10% added to your premium for each 12-month period you went without qualifying coverage. Permanently.

Part D Penalty

1% added for each month you went without drug coverage. This cost stays with you forever.

Most Special Enrollment Periods last **60 days** from the event. Acting quickly is essential to avoiding these extra costs.
